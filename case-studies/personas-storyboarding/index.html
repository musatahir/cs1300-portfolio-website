Evaluating User Interaction with the Quench Water Dispenser Interface

Context / Problem

The Data Science Initiative (DSI) at Brown University previously used a water dispenser that lacked durability and was prone to leakage. To address these issues, the staff replaced it with a new Quench water dispenser, designed to be more reliable and touch-free for improved hygiene and ease of use. The Quench dispenser offers four water optionsâ€”cold, ambient, sparkling, and hot. Instead of physical buttons, users hover their hand over sensors to toggle between selections and dispense water.

Audience

This design focuses on users of the DSI water dispenser, including students, faculty, and staff at Brown University. These users range from frequent visitors who rely on the dispenser daily to occasional users who may encounter it during sporadic visits. The analysis particularly considers both tech-savvy users comfortable with modern interfaces and those who prefer traditional, tactile controls.

Interface Analysis

Interface sketch showing the water dispenser's controls and options.

This interface provides efficient access to clean drinking water with multiple options (cold, sparkling, ambient, and hot). The touch-free hover controls reduce the spread of germs, while the mode selector provides flexibility for different water preferences. However, some users struggle with the lack of pressable buttons, as the hover-based controls are not immediately intuitive and may confuse first-time users.

While this upgrade aimed to enhance functionality and hygiene, our study reveals that it may have introduced usability challenges for beginner and rushed users. This project examines how people interact with the new interface though interviews, personas, and a storymap documenting user experiences.

User Interviews

Behavioral Observations

Approach:

  • Users began by placing their bottle on the tray, most did so without even pausing to read instructions.
  • Some took a few seconds to read the instructions and scan the available options before proceeding.
  • Users with people waiting behind them tended to act more quickly.

Mode Selection:

  • Most users did not need to change the setting, as cold water was most popular and set as default.
  • Some users hovered their front hand over the mode selector to toggle between the options, while others used their back hand.
  • Some even unnecessarily touched the button directly, which would sometimes not trigger any interface response.

Dispensing Process:

  • Users showed varied interaction patterns with the dispensing button.
  • Some actively watched the water while filling, others checked their phones.
  • Bottle alignment issues were common initially.

Exit Behavior:

  • Some users wiped the spout before leaving.
  • Others left immediately after collecting their water.

Interview Findings

Usage Frequency & Preferences

Frequent users relied on the dispenser for convenience, especially those nearby, valuing its speed, free access, and water options. Occasional users used it when visiting or thirsty. Many preferred it over other dispensers for faster flow, cold water, and hygienic, touch-free controls.

Interface Expectations

Most expected basic options like hot and cold water. Some were surprised by the inclusion of sparkling and ambient settings, and others wanted clearer, step-by-step instructions.

Ease of Navigation

Frequent users found it intuitive, while new users struggled due to unclear instructions. Many mistook the hover interface for physical buttons, leading to confusion. Some reported unresponsiveness, requiring multiple hovers.

Water Setting Preferences

Cold water was easy to access as the default. Sparkling and ambient took more effort to find. Hot water required toggling through all other modes, making it less efficient.

Comparison to Other Dispensers

Most had used button- or lever-based dispensers before. This one felt more hygienic and modern but less intuitive, especially for first-time users. Mode toggling was a common frustration.

Expectations vs. Experience

Most found the dispenser fast and convenient once they understood how it worked. The touch-free feature was appreciated for hygiene, though some still had concerns and wiped the spout before use.

Suggested Improvements

Users wanted more responsive buttons, clearer hover labels, and direct mode selection rather than toggling. Some preferred physical buttons for quicker interaction.

Reflection

Our study of the Quench water dispenser revealed a trade-off between innovation and usability. While the touch-free controls offer modern hygiene benefits, first-time users like "Confused Cameron" struggle with the lack of clear instructions, and even experienced users like "Holly Hustle" find the multi-step toggling process inefficient. To improve the experience, we recommend implementing direct mode selection, clearer hover indicators, and more responsive controls.
