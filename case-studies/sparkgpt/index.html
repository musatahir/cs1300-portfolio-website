Designing SparkGPT: Accelerating Solar Development through Conversational AI

My Contributions

  • Led Design 1 and the Menu Viewer low-fidelity prototype
  • Designed multiple screens in the high-fidelity prototype
  • Implemented final design revisions
  • Recorded and edited both Loom walkthrough videos

Collaborators: Jasmine Liu, Fuka Ikeda, Max Boonbandansook

Context / Problem

Solar and battery developers spend significant time gathering critical information like zoning rules, permitting hurdles, and historical records. In the United States, there are 3000+ counties, 10,000+ building permit jurisdictions, and more than 2,000 energy-regulating entities—each with their own set of rules [Source: SparkAI].

SparkAI currently compiles this data into static reports: executive summaries with useful information for developers. However, these reports can't capture every data point, and manually navigating them remains inefficient.

To resolve this issue, our team was asked to design SparkGPT: a new interactive search feature inside Spark's web platform that coexists with the report. Using SparkGPT, users can ask nuanced questions and quickly receive verified, cited answers, bridging the gap between raw data and the high-level insights captured in the report.

Audience

Our target users are solar and battery developers: time-constrained professionals who need fast, trustworthy answers from complex regulatory data. They rely on Spark's executive reports but need more interactivity and source traceability to streamline decision-making.

Project Goals

  • • Explore Design Directions: Brainstorm and evaluate multiple interfaces for SparkGPT and its integration with SparkAI's existing report system
  • • Build Interactive Prototypes:
    • Create a basic low-fidelity prototype
    • Develop an interactive high-fidelity prototype to demonstrate functionality
  • • Support Source Transparency: Ensure SparkGPT responses include clear, clickable citations
  • • Balance Functionality and Usability: Design for both efficiency and clarity, especially for time-constrained solar developers

Early Exploration

We sketched four possible designs for the SparkGPT feature:

User Workflows

  • Option 1: SparkGPT integrated into Reports, linking each chat to a specific report
  • Option 2: SparkGPT as a standalone feature on the SparkAI site

Layout Concepts

  • Explored side-by-side and top-bottom layouts for chat + report views
  • Considered citation depth and how to make them explorable

Design Philosophy

Home Screen Designs:

  • Design #2: Information-dense landing
  • Designs #1 & #4: Modern, minimalist aesthetics, matching SparkAI's current branding
  • Final decision: Minimalist enhancement to maintain product cohesion

Chat Screens:

  • AI-like interface with suggestive prompts
  • Split-screen version for transparency of citations
  • Design #3 introduced a subtle search button, which was later revised based on client confusion around the metaphor

Final Interactive Design

Following a design review with SparkAI, we refined our approach with these key features:

Dual Entry Points:

  • Homepage ➝ Choose report ➝ Launch SparkGPT
  • From a report ➝ Click chat icon to launch SparkGPT side-by-side

Enhanced Features:

  • Searchable Chat Logs: Enables quick recall of prior queries
  • Clickable Citations: Direct links to the exact text inside source documents

Reflections from Feedback

Citation Rendering & Adaptability

  • The clean, clickable citation UI was well-received.
  • Future: Adapt citation display dynamically when a user switches between cities, perhaps by filtering or grouping sources by location.

Button to Launch/Close SparkGPT

  • Users found the top-right open/close icon intuitive for entering and exiting SparkGPT.
  • Future: Explore a persistent toggle in the navigation bar so users can access the chat from anywhere in the report.

Scope of Search Feature

  • Currently, SparkGPT is tied to a single locality, which keeps results focused.
  • Future: Offer a user flow where users launch SparkGPT before selecting any specific report first.

Suggested Questions on Chat Screen

  • Suggested-question buttons smooth the first-time experience by guiding users toward meaningful queries.
  • Future: Personalize those suggestions based on user expertise (e.g., "Getting Started" for new users, "Advanced Filters" for power users).

Leveraging PDF Mental Models

  • Users expected PDF-style interactions: scrolling, pagination, and inline annotations.
  • Future: Mimic these affordances in the web app to lower the barrier of entry, such as adding page-like navigation or hover annotations.

Closing Thoughts

Our experience designing the SparkGPT feature challenged us to think deeply about how UI design choices can shape usability and trust. In particular, our final meeting with the SparkAI team initiated important discussion around how to handle citation granularity, entry points into the chat interface, and the global scope of chats.

For an early iteration of SparkGPT, constraining users through the UI—such as by providing suggested queries and limiting chats to specific reports—helps reduce the need for users to make complex decisions. Looking ahead, SparkGPT could expand its scope through a few exciting future directions:

  • Inline citations that allow users to verify query responses by sentence.
  • Support for queries across multiple locations instead of one town or county at a time.
  • Implementation of features like global vs. per-location chat history.

We are grateful to SparkAI for the opportunity to contribute hands-on to the development of SparkGPT. Special thanks to Julia Wu and Tae Kim for their meaningful insights into the field of renewable energy and their thoughtful feedback on our design iterations.

Moving forward, we are excited about the possibilities ahead in the future of solar development!
